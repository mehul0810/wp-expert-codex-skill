# Enterprise Product Writing

Use this reference for enterprise product pages, buyer-facing WordPress product messaging, security/privacy/governance pages, case studies, comparisons, and WordPress VIP positioning. It controls message architecture and evidence, not visual design.

## Writing Contract

Enterprise writing should let an executive understand the outcome and risk quickly while giving evaluators enough mechanism, control, compatibility, and evidence to verify the claim.

Start with a compact brief:

```text
Primary buyer or role:
Job, friction, or risk:
Desired outcome:
Product mechanism:
Available proof:
Controls and limitations:
Conversion action:
```

If the mechanism or proof is unknown, do not replace it with stronger adjectives. Research it, qualify the claim, or mark the evidence gap.

## Message Unit

Build each material section from the smallest useful combination of:

1. **Audience:** the accountable role, team, industry, or use case.
2. **Problem:** costly friction, operational risk, or blocked outcome.
3. **Outcome:** the specific improvement the reader values.
4. **Mechanism:** what the shipped product actually does to produce it.
5. **Proof:** product evidence, customer result, measurement, or named authority.
6. **Control:** permissions, governance, integration, recovery, or admin behavior.
7. **Limit:** eligibility, dependency, assumption, or condition that changes the claim.
8. **Action:** the next step and its commitment level.

Do not force all eight into every paragraph. A hero may use outcome, mechanism, proof, and action; a security section needs control, limit, and evidence.

## Two-Speed Reading

Support two readers on the same page:

- **Executive layer:** concise promise, accountable business outcome, risk reduction, meaningful proof, and next action.
- **Evaluator layer:** workflow, integration, compatibility, administration, security/privacy, scale behavior, failure handling, implementation detail, and documentation.

Headings should carry the decision path. Body copy should explain how the claim works, not restate the heading.

## Evidence And Claim Discipline

Prefer evidence in this order:

1. Verified shipped behavior, runtime proof, or current product documentation.
2. Named customer result with scope, timeframe, and measurement context.
3. First-party aggregate with methodology and qualification.
4. Named independent certification, benchmark, analyst source, or research.
5. Named customer quotation tied to a concrete workflow.

Logos, adoption counts, testimonials, and awards are trust signals, not proof of every product claim. Keep evidence close to the claim it supports.

Never invent customer names, metrics, rankings, certifications, scale, savings, uptime, security posture, or comparative superiority. Words such as `best`, `fastest`, `only`, `leader`, and `enterprise-grade` require current evidence and a defined comparison. When results vary, state the relevant condition or range.

## Artifact Tone

- **Hero:** compressed, confident, outcome-first; one primary action.
- **Product section:** outcome, mechanism, operational consequence, proof.
- **Security/privacy:** literal, precise, qualified, non-promotional; answer ownership, access, retention, encryption, audit, and failure questions.
- **Case study:** customer context, prior constraint, implementation, measured result, attribution, limitation.
- **Comparison:** consistent criteria, sourced differences, tradeoffs, and unknowns.
- **Documentation:** procedural, terminology-consistent, and direct.
- **CTA:** describe the next action precisely: view demo, validate compatibility, read architecture, start trial, or contact sales.

Use calm confidence. Prefer concrete nouns and verbs over category slogans, decorative futurism, and unsupported urgency. Enterprise language should not make a simple product sound bureaucratic.

## AI And Automated Workflows

Lead with the governed workflow, not model novelty. State where human approval is required, which permissions and data sources apply, whether data is retained or used for training, what actions are logged, how reliability is measured, and what happens when automation fails or needs review. Do not imply autonomous authority, privacy, or accuracy beyond verified behavior.

## Page Sequence

For a buyer-facing page, prefer:

```text
Outcome-led hero
Immediate credible proof
Role/job/risk framing
Mechanism and workflow
Controls, integration, and failure behavior
Customer or operational evidence
Objection-handling FAQ
Specific next action
```

Adapt the sequence to intent. Do not add empty sections merely to complete a template.

## Final Gate

- Can the buyer identify who the page is for and what changes?
- Does every material outcome have a mechanism or explicit evidence gap?
- Are metrics, superlatives, security claims, and customer claims source-backed?
- Can an evaluator find controls, compatibility, limitations, and failure behavior?
- Is the copy simple enough for a non-technical reader without hiding technical truth?
- Does the CTA match the reader's likely evaluation stage?
- Are planned capabilities excluded from present-tense claims?
